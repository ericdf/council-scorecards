Incident Record · December 11, 2024
Howard Johnson Motel — Community Meeting Three Weeks After Council Vote
Sources: Tregub constituent email, Dec. 11, 2024 (ITregub@berkeleyca.gov);
Agenda record 2024-11-19 (council vote approving Dorothy Day House contract)
Igor Tregub · District 4
What Happened

On November 19, 2024, the Berkeley City Council voted to approve a contract with Dorothy Day House to operate the Howard Johnson Motel in the Willard neighborhood (District 4) as interim supportive housing. The vote was a formal council action establishing the operational framework for the project.

On December 11, 2024 — three weeks after the vote — Tregub sent a constituent email announcing a community meeting on the Howard Johnson project. The email acknowledged the council action explicitly:

"By way of background, at its November 19 meeting, the City Council approved a contract with Dorothy Day House to operate the Howard Johnson Motel as interim housing..."

The meeting was framed as a community conversation about the project. But the operative question — whether the city would proceed with Dorothy Day House as the operator — had already been answered. The meeting could inform implementation details but could not change the contract the council had already authorized.

Post-Decision Community Meeting Dressed as Engagement
Character & Conduct
−8

Genuine constituent engagement on a significant land use and social services siting decision happens before the council vote: gathering community input, surfacing concerns, and bringing those concerns into the deliberation. Post-decision meetings serve a legitimate but more limited purpose — explaining what was decided and how it will be implemented. The distinction matters, and Tregub's email elides it by framing a notification meeting as a conversation.

Residents who attended expecting to influence the outcome would have had their time wasted on a decision that was already final. Residents who might have wanted to weigh in before the vote had no comparable opportunity organized by their council member. The procedural asymmetry — deliberation without public input, notification dressed as dialogue — is the governance failure that this score reflects.

This is a moderate-severity incident because the engagement, while post-decision, was at least held. A council member who sited an unpopular facility in their district and held no meeting at all would warrant a higher negative score. The failure here is sequencing and framing, not absence of constituent contact.

Net Incident Score
−8
Affects: Character & Conduct pillar; Incident Record pillar.
Decay policy: Full weight from December 2024. Reduces 20% per year. Resets if a comparable post-decision notification is documented for a significant district land use or services decision.
