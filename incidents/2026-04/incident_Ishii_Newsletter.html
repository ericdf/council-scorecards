Mayor's Monthly: April 2026 — Attendance Log, Not Leadership
Source: Mayor Adena Ishii newsletter "The Mayor's Monthly: April 2026," April 27, 2026 (mayor@berkeleyca.gov)
Adena Ishii · Mayor
What Happened

The April 2026 Mayor's Monthly newsletter opens with a staged photo of the mayor with an Easter Bunny pop-up — the kind of social-media-ready image that functions as personal visibility content rather than civic communication. The photo has no informational value to residents: no local business is being celebrated, no children's program is being highlighted, no community partner's work is being amplified. It is a picture of the mayor existing in public, dressed up as a community moment.

The rest of the newsletter follows the same pattern. Every substantive item reports something Ishii attended rather than something she drove. The wildfire vegetation cleanup — organized by BerkeleyCorps, Berkeley Firewise, and HelpBerkeley — is genuinely important civic work. The newsletter mentions it without inviting readers to the next one, linking to any of the organizing groups, or naming what residents can do before fire season. It is a lookback with no forward motion.

A questionnaire asks Berkeley businesses to self-identify as potential World Cup viewing venues. This implies Visit Berkeley — whose core function is to maintain relationships with Berkeley businesses — does not already know which venues might host game viewings. A tourism bureau with an active business network would reach out directly; publishing a general questionnaire signals the network is thin or the idea originated without it.

The budget section reprints substantially the same statement Ishii issued before the public input meetings, with no acknowledgment of what she heard or how her thinking evolved. Community meetings are presented, by omission, as a formality the mayor attended rather than a source of information that changed anything.

Communication as Self-Promotion
Character & Conduct
−3

A mayor's newsletter is a public resource — staff time, city infrastructure, a subscriber list built on the office. Using the opening of that newsletter for a photo that serves no civic purpose is a small but telling misallocation. The Easter Bunny image is the newsletter equivalent of a vacation photo: it signals presence and approachability but contributes nothing to residents trying to understand what their mayor is doing or how to engage with city government.

The deeper problem is structural. The entire newsletter reports attendance. Ishii was at the art reception. She was at the wildfire cleanup. She was at various community events. None of these items ask the reader to do anything, support anything, or change anything. A mayor's newsletter that reads as a social calendar is not governing communication — it is brand maintenance.

Budget Input — No Evidence of Evolution
Character & Conduct
−3

Berkeley held public input sessions on the 2027–2028 budget. The April newsletter's budget section contains no signal that those sessions changed Ishii's thinking in any way. No "here is what I heard." No "this surprised me." No acknowledgment that the input process produced information. The statement reads as though it was drafted before the meetings occurred.

Community input that produces no visible output is not community engagement — it is community process. The distinction matters: residents who attend input sessions to influence outcomes need to see evidence that their participation mattered. A newsletter sent after those sessions is an obvious place to provide that evidence. This one does not.
