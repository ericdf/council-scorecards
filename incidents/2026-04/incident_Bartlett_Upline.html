Household Entanglement with City Subcontractor
Source: San Francisco Chronicle, Kate Talerico, April 22, 2026
Ben Bartlett · District 3
What Happened

Between 2022 and 2024, the city of Berkeley paid $607,000 to Berkeley Youth Alternatives and its subcontractor, Upline Solutions, for a cannabis education program funded by a Proposition 64 state grant. The program failed to deliver its core obligations: the advertising campaign Upline was hired to create was never produced; the peer education program trained students but never delivered peer-led sessions to other students; and the community task force launched nearly two years late. The California Board of State and Community Corrections nonetheless certified that Berkeley had "met all grant obligations."

State filings show that Upline Solutions was incorporated in October 2019 by Patricia Brooks and Yelda Bartlett, wife of District 3 Councilmember Ben Bartlett. The company listed its registered address as Yelda Bartlett's law office in downtown Oakland. Kevin Jenkins — now Oakland City Council President and Brooks' current employer — was also listed as a registered agent of the company when it formed.

Bartlett said he was unaware his wife had filed the incorporation paperwork and that he had no role in helping Brooks obtain the contract. He described himself as "disappointed and concerned" by the program's failures. When asked about accountability, he said: "Maybe there's a world where they could still fulfill the missing elements" — of a contract whose performance period had ended in 2024.

Conflict of Interest & Recusal
Character & Conduct
−13

Bartlett's household was financially connected — through his wife's incorporation of the subcontractor and use of her professional office as the company's registered address — to a company that received city money during his tenure. No public disclosure of this connection has been identified in council records or meeting transcripts. No recusal from related votes has been identified.

Two distinct failures compound each other here. Disclosure is the threshold obligation: telling the public and fellow council members that the connection exists. Recusal is the complete remedy: not voting on anything touching an entangled contractor. Bartlett did neither. Had he only disclosed, his office would have carried an ongoing burden to identify every BYA- or Upline-adjacent vote and manage each appropriately — a real administrative cost against the constituent work his district deserves. Recusal would have been the cleaner path.

Bartlett has served on the Berkeley City Council longer than any of his current colleagues. He is not navigating disclosure and recusal obligations for the first time, and cannot claim an ingenue's unfamiliarity with the rules. The severity of this failure scales with the experience he brings to it.

Quality of Public Response
Character & Conduct
−5

When presented with documented evidence of the program's failures and his household's connection to the subcontractor, Bartlett said he was "disappointed and concerned" and suggested the contractors might yet complete work from a closed grant period. He proposed no review process, no attempt to recover funds for undelivered work, and no acknowledgment of his disclosure or recusal obligations.

A member exercising genuine fiscal accountability would have identified what remedies he would pursue and what his office had failed to catch. A member protecting a relationship said what Bartlett said. These are not the same thing, and the difference is legible in the record.
