American Renewal Plan — Federal Policy Framework on District Office Platform
Source: American Renewal Plan, published on Bartlett council office platform (captured April 12, 2026)
Ben Bartlett · District 3
What Happened

Bartlett published the "American Renewal Plan" — a five-pillar national economic reform framework — on his Berkeley City Council office platform. The document is addressed to "Policymakers, Legislative Staff, Economic Advisors" and proposes legislation at the federal level. Its five pillars are: a federal mandate for a 32-hour work week; condo-ization of all U.S. public housing stock; an American Resource Dividend (a direct payment to citizens funded by natural resource revenues); making credit-card interest payments tax-deductible; and a Modern Debt Jubilee. None of these items fall within Berkeley's authority, address Berkeley's documented P1 failures, or are otherwise connected to the office from which they were published.

Berkeley's current documented problems include a structural fiscal deficit described by City Manager staff as "not sustainable," streets underfunded by an estimated $42M per year (per the City Auditor's Rocky Road report), CalPERS and OPEB obligations that have not been structurally addressed, and reserves that have been drawn down across multiple budget cycles. Bartlett is the longest-serving member of the current council and was one of the co-authors of the 2023 council staffing expansion passed on the consent calendar during the same deficit period.

Scope Indiscipline — Jurisdiction and Priority
Character & Conduct
−10

Publishing a federal economic reform framework from a city council office platform is the most extreme scope departure in the current council's record. The American Renewal Plan does not advance any item within Berkeley's legal authority. It does not redirect federal funding toward Berkeley. It does not engage any Berkeley Priority 1 (P1) problem. It is a policy proposal for other governments, published as if it were the work of a District 3 council member.

The severity here is not that Bartlett has ambitious ideas about national policy — council members are entitled to personal views. The severity is that the platform he used to publish this is the same platform his District 3 constituents use to learn about local services, permits, neighborhood concerns, and his votes. The attention and staff capacity he applied to authoring and publishing a five-pillar federal framework were not available for Berkeley's unresolved local failures. This is not a difficult prioritization call: a council member has a formal legal obligation to the residents of his district, and a zero formal legal obligation to redesign federal labor or housing policy.

Bartlett's seniority compounds the severity. He is the council's longest-serving member. He has observed the structural deficit accumulate across multiple city managers and multiple budget cycles without authoring a structural solution. The American Renewal Plan is the most recent of a pattern: engaging energetically with jurisdictions and audiences other than Berkeley while Berkeley's structural problems remain unaddressed.
