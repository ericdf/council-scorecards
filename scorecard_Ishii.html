The Office: Powers, Constraints, and What Accountability Requires
The Mayor of Berkeley holds powers unavailable to district council members: chairs all regular and special council sessions, helps shape and prioritize the meeting agenda through the Agenda Committee, makes key appointments to boards and commissions, and serves as the city’s principal public voice through official communications and proclamations. Within the council–manager system defined by the Berkeley City Charter, these authorities do not include control of city operations or unilateral control of the agenda.
The mayor’s effective power depends on coalition-building. With a reliable majority, agenda influence becomes actionable: items are scheduled promptly, framed in alignment with council priorities, and brought to a vote with predictable outcomes. Without that majority, the same tools are limited to sequencing and emphasis — able to delay or highlight issues, but not to decide them. In this structure, the mayor is not a gatekeeper but a coordinator of votes.
Because the structural deficit and infrastructure backlog are the city’s documented fiscal constraints, a mayor who builds and maintains a coalition to force those tradeoffs onto the agenda and through to decisions is exercising the core function of the office. A mayor who does not assemble that majority, and instead fills the calendar with symbolic or ceremonial items while leaving fiscal choices to the default budget process, is not using the office’s primary lever.
This scorecard therefore asks two questions. First: did Ishii use her platform and agenda access to assemble a working majority around Berkeley’s documented fiscal problems? Second: does the record of what she scheduled, communicated, and decided reflect that priority — or something else? Ishii’s 91.7% block-vote rate is the highest on the council, confirming she votes with the majority consistently. But voting with a majority and building one around specific fiscal goals are different things. A coalition formed around low-friction consensus items is not evidence of governing effectiveness; it is evidence of conflict avoidance. The 58-session record evaluated here (2024-12-10–2026-03-24) is the evidence.

Of 53 items Ishii authored or cosponsored across the analysis period, 0 address a documented P1 fiscal or infrastructure problem. All 7 P1-class items on Berkeley’s agendas during this period were brought by the City Manager or staff — the default budget process the mayor’s coalition authority exists to supplement and direct. Whether that reflects a judgment that the coalition wasn’t available, or a preference not to make those fights, the effect is the same: the office’s primary lever was not applied to its highest-priority purpose.

3 · Meeting Management  (25% of Mayoral Accountability score)
The mayor chairs council meetings and sets the pace. When meetings require extension votes — formal motions to continue past the scheduled end time — it is a ground-truth signal that the agenda exceeded what a governing body can reasonably process. Decisions made at midnight, under sustained public pressure, after hours of contentious comment, are not the same decisions that would be made with adequate deliberation. Extended meetings also close democratic participation: ordinary residents with jobs and families cannot wait until 1 AM to speak.

4 · Accountability Gap  (15% of Mayoral Accountability score)
Ishii ran for mayor in a cycle when Measure II — creating the Police Accountability Board (PAB) and Office of the Director of Police Accountability (ODPA) — passed with 85% of the vote. Under her mayoralty, the institution voters mandated did not survive in functional form.
TIMELINE OF INSTITUTIONAL COLLAPSE
2020–2026 All original PAB commissioners departed. Board dropped to 4 of 9 seats.
Jan 30, 2026 Last two original commissioners resigned, writing the PAB was “even less empowered than its predecessor” — the one the DOJ had cited as a national model.
Dec 2025 ODPA Director Aguilar sued Police Chief Jen Louis to compel records release. BPD had refused records requests, requiring multiple subpoenas.
Sep 2025 Aguilar placed items on council agenda using his charter authority — the authority voters designed him to have. Council members publicly criticized him for using it.
Feb 9, 2026 Council voted 8–0 (Kesarwani absent) to fire Aguilar under “without cause” contract terms, triggering mandatory severance.
Post-vote statement — Ishii: “The council remained committed to police accountability and was focused on filling the PAB vacancies, finding a new director and restoring credibility, trust and respect for both the accountability process and the people involved in this work.” — issued immediately after the unanimous vote to eliminate the accountability office’s leadership. A recommitment-to-accountability statement issued in the moment the accountability office’s director was fired is the definitional form of performative governance. (Source: Berkeleyside, Alex N.
